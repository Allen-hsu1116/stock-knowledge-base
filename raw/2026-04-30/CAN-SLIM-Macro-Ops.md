William O'Neil is a highly successful investor and businessman who is the definition of an American success story. Born in Oklahoma during the Great Depression and raised in Texas, O'Neil started his financial career as a stockbroker for Hayden, Stone and Company in 1958. By pyramiding the profits in three exceptional back-to-back trades during 1962-1963, he managed to turn an initial $5,000 investment into $200,000.

O'Neil went on to use his investment winnings to buy a seat on the New York Stock Exchange and to form William O'Neil and Co., an institutional research brokerage firm. He also launched Investor's Business Daily in direct competition to the Wall Street Journal, all while maintaining his impressive track record as a stock investor — averaging over a 40% annually on his investments.

## CAN SLIM 七字母詳解

### C: Current Earnings（當前季度獲利）

The best-performing stocks showed a 70 percent average increase in earnings for the current quarter over the same quarter in the prior year before they began their major advance. Quarterly earnings per share should be up by at least 20 to 50 percent year to year.

Examples:
- Dell's earnings surged 74% and 108% before its 587% price run in 61 weeks
- Cisco posted gains of 150% and 155% before its significant run-up
- America Online's earnings were up 900% and 283% before its 451% burst
- Google showed gains of 112% and 123% before its spectacular debut
- Apple's earnings increased 350% then 300%, stock went from $12 to $202

### A: Annual Earnings（年度獲利）

The prior five-year average annual compounded earnings growth rate of outstanding performing stocks at their early emerging stage was 24 percent. Ideally, each year's earnings per share should show an increase over the prior year's earnings. O'Neil recommends looking for annual EPS that has increased for the last three consecutive years.

Examples:
- Xerox grew at 32% annual rate before 700% rise
- Wal-Mart had 43% annual growth before 11,200% run from 1977 to 1990
- Priceline.com doubled earnings from $0.96 to $2.03 before tripling stock price
- Google expanded earnings from $0.55 to $2.51 before stock climbed from $200 to $700

### N: New Products, Management, or Markets（新產品、新管理、新市場）

95 percent of the greatest winners had something new. The "new" also refers to a new high price for the stock. 98 percent of investors are unwilling to buy a stock at a new high, yet it is one of the great paradoxes of the stock market that what seems too high usually goes higher, and what seems too low usually goes lower.

Examples:
- Northern Pacific railroad: 4,000% in 197 weeks
- McDonald's franchising: 1,100% from 1967 to 1971
- Cisco Systems: nearly 2,000% from 1990 to 1994, then 75,000% from 1990 to 2000

### S: Shares Outstanding / Supply and Demand（流通股數/供需）

95% of the best-performing stocks had less than 25 million shares of capitalization during their best performance period. Average capitalization was 11.8 million shares, median was only 4.6 million. Companies that consistently buy back their own stock are usually a good bet. Fewer shares = less buying required to send prices higher.

### L: Leader or Laggard（領先股或落後股）

The 500 best-performing stocks during 1953-1985 had an average relative strength of 87 before their major price increase. A relative strength of 80 means the stock outperformed 80% of all other stocks during the past year. O'Neil restricts purchases to companies with relative strength ranks above 80.

Example: Home Depot went from $0.59 to $11 (1766% increase) as a leader in retail.
Counter-example: IBM was a laggard in 1979-1980, dropping from $18.28 to $12.24.

### I: Institutional Sponsorship（法人持股）

Institutional buyers are the largest source of demand for stocks. While some institutional sponsorship is desired, excessive sponsorship is not — it could lead to significant selling if anything goes wrong. By the time almost all institutions own a stock, it is probably too late to buy.

Example: Xerox was placed on the sell list at $115 in 1974, but institutions kept buying, leading to significant losses.

### M: Market Direction（大盤方向）

Three out of four stocks will go in the same direction as a significant move in the market averages. It is essential to learn how to interpret price and volume on a daily basis for signs that the market has topped. Investing when the broader market is making new highs is a great bet. But when the market is falling, don't expect to pick winners.

## The Perfect CAN SLIM Trade

- C: Quarterly EPS up at least 20% YoY
- A: 5-year average earnings growth rate minimum 24%, annual EPS increased for last 3 consecutive years
- N: New product, service, management, or stock price making new high
- S: Market cap below 25 million shares
- L: Leader in its sector and industry
- I: Some institutional interest
- M: General market in uptrend
