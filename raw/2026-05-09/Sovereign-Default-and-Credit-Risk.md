# Sovereign Default and Sovereign Credit Risk

**來源**：https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_default, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereign_risk
**日期**：2026-05-09
**主題**：風險管理

---

A sovereign default is the failure or refusal of the government of a sovereign state to pay back its debt in full when due. Cessation of due payments may either be accompanied by formal declaration that it will not pay (repudiation), or it may be unannounced.

Countries have at times escaped some of the real burden of their debt through inflation. This is not "default" in the usual sense because the debt is honored, albeit with currency of lesser real value. Sometimes governments devalue their currency by printing more money or by altering the convertibility of their currencies.

If potential lenders or bond purchasers begin to suspect that a government may fail to pay back its debt, they may demand a high interest rate in compensation for the risk of default. A dramatic rise in the interest rate faced by a government due to fear that it will fail to honor its debt is sometimes called a sovereign debt crisis.

Governments may be especially vulnerable to a sovereign debt crisis when they rely on financing through short-term bonds, since this creates a maturity mismatch between their short-term bond financing and the long-term asset value of their tax base. They may also be vulnerable due to currency mismatch: if few bonds in their own currency are accepted abroad, and so the country issues mainly foreign currency-denominated bonds, a decrease in the value of their own currency can make it prohibitively expensive to pay back those bonds (see original sin).

Five key factors that affect the probability of sovereign debt leading to sovereign risk are: debt service ratio, import ratio, investment ratio, variance of export revenue, and domestic money supply growth. The probability of loss increases with increases in debt service ratio, import ratio, variance of export revenue and/or domestic money supply growth.

According to financial historian Edward Chancellor, past instances of sovereign default have tended to occur under some or all of the following circumstances:
- A reversal of global capital flows
- Unwise lending
- Fraudulent lending
- Excessive foreign debts
- A poor credit history
- Unproductive lending
- Rollover risk
- Weak revenues
- Rising interest rates
- Terminal debt

### Insolvency vs Illiquidity

There is an important distinction between illiquidity and insolvency. If a country is temporarily unable to meet pending interest or principle payments because it cannot liquify sufficient assets, it is "in default because of illiquidity". The default can be solved as soon as the assets become liquid again, in contrast to insolvency. The weakness of this concept is that it is practically impossible to prove that an asset is only temporarily illiquid.

### Change of Government

After a regime change the new government may question the legitimacy of the earlier one, and thus default on those treasury obligations considered odious debt. Examples include:
- Default of debts of the House of Bourbon after the French Revolution
- Default of debts of the Russian Empire after the Soviet government came to power in 1917
- Repudiation of debts of the Confederate States by the United States after the Civil War

### Approaches to Debt Repayment

**Reputation approach**: Countries value access to international capital markets because it allows them to smooth consumption. Debtor countries with poor reputations will lack access to these capital markets.

**Punishment approach**: The debtor will be punished in some form, whether by legal action and/or military force. The punishment may prevent debtors from being able to borrow in their own currency.

### Consequences for Creditors

The immediate cost to creditors is the loss of principal and interest owed. Very often there are international negotiations that end in partial debt cancellation or debt restructuring (e.g., Brady Bonds in the 1980s, London Agreement on German External Debts 1953). In the Argentine economic crisis (1999–2002), some creditors accepted haircuts of up to 75% of outstanding debts.

### Consequences for the State

When a state defaults, it disposes of its financial obligations toward certain creditors. The immediate effect is a reduction in total debt and a reduction in payments on interest. However, the state may be widely excluded from further credit, some overseas assets may be seized, and it may face political pressure from domestic bondholders.

IMF often lends for sovereign debt restructuring, making loans conditional on actions such as reducing corruption, imposing austerity measures, or raising tax revenue.
