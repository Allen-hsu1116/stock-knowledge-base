# Financial Contagion

**來源**：https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_contagion
**日期**：2026-05-09
**主題**：風險管理

---

Financial contagion refers to "the spread of market disturbances—mostly on the downside—from one country to the other, a process observed through co-movements in exchange rates, stock prices, sovereign spreads, and capital flows" (Dornbusch et al.).

Financial contagion can be a potential risk for countries who are trying to integrate their financial system with international financial markets and institutions. It helps explain an economic crisis extending across neighboring countries, or even regions.

Financial contagion happens at both the international level and the domestic level. At the domestic level, usually the failure of a domestic bank or financial intermediary triggers transmission when it defaults on interbank liabilities and sells assets in a fire sale, thereby undermining confidence in similar banks. International financial contagion is the transmission of financial crisis across financial markets for direct or indirect economies.

## Causes and Consequences

### Branch 1: Spillover Effects (Fundamental-based Contagion)

Spillover effects can be seen as negative externalities. These effects can happen either globally or regionally.

**Common Shocks**: Macroeconomic shocks that have repercussions on an international scale. A financial crisis in one country can lead to direct financial effects, including reductions in trade credits, foreign direct investment, and other capital flows abroad.

**Financial Links**: Allen and Gale (2000) provide a general equilibrium model to explain how a small liquidity preference shock in one region can spread by contagion throughout the economy. The possibility of contagion depends strongly on the completeness of the structure of interregional claims.

**Trade Links**: Any major trading partner of a country in which a financial crisis has induced a sharp currency depreciation could experience declining asset prices and large capital outflows or could become the target of a speculative attack as investors anticipate a decline in exports to the crisis country. Kaminsky and Reinhart (2000) document the evidence that trade links and exposure to a common creditor can explain earlier crises clusters.

**Competitive Devaluation**: When multiple countries compete to gain a competitive advantage by having low exchange rates for their currency. Devaluation in a country hit by a crisis reduces the export competitiveness of the countries with which it competes in third markets. If market participants expect a currency crisis will lead to a game of competitive devaluation, they will naturally sell their holdings of securities of other countries.

### Branch 2: Financial Crisis (Irrational Phenomena)

A financial crisis as a branch of contagion is formed when "a co-movement occurs, even when there are no global shocks and interdependence and fundamentals are not factors." It is caused by any of the four agents' behaviors who influence financial globalization: governments, financial institutions, investors, and borrowers.

**Correlated Information Channel**: Price changes in one market are perceived as having implications for the values of assets in other markets (King and Wadhwani, 1990).

**Correlated Liquidity Shock Channel**: When some market participants need to liquidate and withdraw some of their assets to obtain cash, perhaps after experiencing an unexpected loss in another country and need to restore capital adequacy ratios (Calvo, 2004).

### Three Types of Investor Behavior

1. **Rational but excessive co-movements**: Investors take action that is ex-ante individually rational but lead to excessive co-movements that cannot be explained by real fundamentals. Sub-categories:
   - Liquidity and incentive problems: Losses may induce investors to sell off securities in other markets to raise cash.
   - Information asymmetries and coordination problems: One group has more or significantly better information compared to another group.

2. **Multiple equilibria**: Contagion occurs when a crisis in one financial market causes another financial market to move or jump to a bad equilibrium, characterized by a devaluation, a drop in asset prices, capital outflows, or debt default.

3. **Changes in international financial system**: Investors adjust their behaviors after an initial crisis occurs internationally.

### Geographic Factors

De Gregorio and Valdes (2001) examine how the 1982 debt crisis, the 1994 Mexican crisis, and the 1997 Asian crisis spread. They find that a neighborhood effect is the strongest determinant of which countries suffer from contagion. Trade links and pre-crisis growth similarities are also important.

### Historical Examples

The term "contagion" was first introduced in July 1997, when the currency crisis in Thailand quickly spread throughout East Asia and then on to Russia and Brazil. Even developed markets in North America and Europe were affected, as the relative prices of financial instruments shifted and caused the collapse of Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM). The financial crisis beginning from Thailand with the collapse of the Thai baht spread to Indonesia, the Philippines, Malaysia, South Korea and Hong Kong in less than two months.

## Controversy

Some argue that strong linkages between countries are not necessarily financial contagion, and that contagion should be defined as an increase in cross-market linkages after a shock to one country. Some scholars argue there is actually no contagion at all, just a high level of market co-movement in all periods, which is market "interdependence" (Forbes and Rigobon).
