## Risk Budgeting Approach

### What is Risk Budgeting?

Risk budgeting is an approach to portfolio construction that allocates risk rather than capital. The fundamental principle is that the total risk of a portfolio should be divided among its components in a way that maximizes risk-adjusted returns.

### Key Concepts

**Risk Contribution**: Each asset's contribution to total portfolio risk depends on:
1. Its weight in the portfolio
2. Its own volatility
3. Its correlation with other portfolio assets

**The Risk Budgeting Equation**:
For a portfolio with n assets, the risk budget is satisfied when:
w_i × (∂σ_p / ∂w_i) = σ_p × b_i

Where:
- w_i = weight of asset i
- ∂σ_p / ∂w_i = marginal risk contribution
- σ_p = portfolio risk
- b_i = risk budget of asset i
- Σb_i = 1 (budgets sum to 1)

### Steps to Implement Risk Budgeting

**Step 1: Define the Risk Measure**
Choose your risk measure: standard deviation, VaR, CVaR, or tracking error. Standard deviation is most common for its simplicity.

**Step 2: Set the Total Risk Budget**
This is the overall risk tolerance. Example: portfolio volatility = 12%, or VaR(95%) = 8%.

**Step 3: Determine Risk Budget Allocations**
Decide how to distribute risk across components:
- Equal risk contribution: each asset contributes equally to risk
- Proportional to Sharpe ratio: higher Sharpe → more risk budget
- Based on views: more risk budget to higher-conviction trades

**Step 4: Solve for Optimal Weights**
Use numerical optimization to find weights that satisfy the risk budget constraints. The most common algorithm is the CCD (Cyclical Coordinate Descent) algorithm by Griveau-Billion et al. (2013).

**Step 5: Monitor and Rebalance**
Risk contributions drift over time as correlations and volatilities change. Rebalance when:
- Any asset's ACTR deviates > 20% from target
- Portfolio volatility exceeds the budget
- Correlation regime shift detected

### Equal Risk Contribution (ERC) vs Risk Parity

ERC is a special case of risk budgeting where all assets have equal risk budgets (b_i = 1/n for all i). Risk parity extends this by allowing different risk budgets.

**ERC Portfolio Characteristics**:
- Low volatility assets get higher capital weights
- High volatility assets get lower capital weights
- Tends to outperform in risk-off environments
- Underweights equities relative to traditional 60/40

### Risk Budgeting with Views

When you have specific views on asset returns, you can combine risk budgeting with the Black-Litterman framework:
1. Start with risk budget weights
2. Adjust using view confidence levels
3. High-conviction views get more risk budget
4. Low-conviction views get less risk budget

### Practical Example: Multi-Asset Portfolio

Assets: Equities, Bonds, Commodities, Gold

Traditional 60/40:
- Equities: 60% capital, ~90% risk
- Bonds: 40% capital, ~10% risk

Risk Budget (equal risk):
- Equities: ~30% capital, 25% risk
- Bonds: ~50% capital, 25% risk
- Commodities: ~12% capital, 25% risk
- Gold: ~8% capital, 25% risk

Key insight: In a 60/40 portfolio, equities dominate risk. Risk budgeting forces diversification of risk sources.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Ignoring correlation shifts**: Correlations spike in crises, making budgets obsolete
2. **Over-reliance on historical data**: Tail risk is underestimated
3. **Not stress-testing**: Always test under extreme scenarios
4. **Confusing capital weight with risk weight**: They are very different
5. **Static budgets in dynamic markets**: Risk budgets should adapt to regime changes
