## What is SOTP?

Sum of the Parts Analysis (SOTP), or "break-up analysis", estimates the value of each business segment within a company separately, which are then added together to arrive at the company's implied total enterprise value.

## How to Perform SOTP Valuation

The SOTP valuation is most appropriate for valuing companies with multiple divisions that are each distinct from one another from a risk/return standpoint, creating the need to "break up" the company into separate components for the valuation to be more accurate.

For companies suitable for a SOTP valuation, under the DCF approach, each of their segments would adhere to a different discount rate, which means the expected returns (and coinciding risks) of each individual segment would differ.

If attempting to value the company through multiples analysis, it'll be quite challenging to determine a single appropriate trading or transaction multiple, considering just how widespread the implied ranges will be across the different business segments.

## SOTP Valuation Method Structure (Step-by-Step)

- Step 1: Identify the Appropriate Business Segments
- Step 2: Perform Standalone Valuations of Each Segment (Comps, DCF)
- Step 3: Add-Up the Calculated Valuations for the Total Enterprise Value (TEV)
- Step 4: Subtract Net Debt and Non-Operating Items from TEV

From the total enterprise value (TEV), or "total firm value", the company's total net debt (gross debt less cash and equivalents) yields the implied equity value, i.e. the residual value attributable to only common shareholders.

By dividing the implied equity value by the total number of shares outstanding, we arrive at the SOTP-derived share price, which can then be compared to the current pricing in the open markets to determine if the shares are undervalued, overvalued, or fairly valued.

## SOTP Formula

Total Enterprise Value = Σ(Value of Each Segment)

Equity Value = Total Enterprise Value - Net Debt - Non-Operating Liabilities + Non-Operating Assets

SOTP Value Per Share = Equity Value / Diluted Shares Outstanding

## When to Use SOTP Analysis?

- Conglomerates: The most common reason to use SOTP analysis is to perform valuation analyses on companies with multiple business segments operating in different industries.
- Restructuring: The SOTP can also be useful in restructuring, where distressed companies identify underperforming, non-core business segments that could be sold to increase liquidity.
- Spin-Offs: Another frequent use-case is for divestitures, such as spin-offs. The question: "Is the whole greater than the sum of its parts?" If yes, the subsidiary would be better off remaining part of the parent company. If no, the subsidiary could be in a more favorable position if spun off.

## SOTP Valuation Example: Biotech Sector

One sector in which SOTP analysis is relied upon is the biotech sector, particularly for clinical-stage, pre-revenue companies. Performing a valuation on a biotech company requires a wide range of assumptions per therapeutic asset – such as the estimated market size, revenue potential ("peak opportunity") and uptake curve post-product launch – as well as the probability of success (POS) rate.

## Limitations of SOTP Valuation

- Limited Segment-Level Data: Public companies, including conglomerates, rarely provide sufficient information in their filings to build a complete model for each segment.
- Reliance on Broad Assumptions: The difficulty in compiling the required information can force broader assumptions, making the valuation range less credible.
- Treatment of Synergies: The synergies that result across divisions such as cost savings benefiting each segment cannot be isolated nor easily distributed across business segments.

## SOTP Conglomerate Example: Berkshire Hathaway

SOTP valuations are often used when the target has several operating divisions in unrelated industries, each with different risk profiles. Berkshire Hathaway is a classic example with insurance, railroad (BNSF), energy, manufacturing/service/retail, and investment portfolio divisions.

## Modeling Example

Segment A: EBITDA = $100M, EV/EBITDA 6.0x-8.0x → TEV $600M-$800M
Segment B: EBITDA = $20M, EV/EBITDA 14.0x-20.0x → TEV $280M-$400M
Segment C: EBITDA = $10M, EV/EBITDA 18.0x-24.0x → TEV $180M-$240M

Total TEV: Low $1.06B, High $1.44B
Net Debt: $200M
Implied Equity Value: Low $860M, High $1.24B
