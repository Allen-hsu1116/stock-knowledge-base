Sum-of-parts valuation is the essential methodology for discovering hidden value in diversified companies. This technique reveals that conglomerates often trade at 13-15% discounts to their intrinsic break-up value. SOTP values each business segment independently and then sums them, making it a critical approach for investors analysing stocks such as Berkshire Hathaway, Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta.

The transformative power of SOTP analysis became evident when General Electric's 2024 break-up unlocked 4× the value relative to its pre-split market cap.

## When to use SOTP for conglomerate valuation

SOTP valuation, also called break-up analysis, operates on a simple but powerful premise. Different business segments within a conglomerate have distinct growth rates, risk profiles, and industry dynamics that a single company-wide approach fails to capture.

When Amazon trades as a single entity, the market may apply a retail multiple to the entire business. However, AWS deserves a cloud-computing multiple of 8x revenue, while the advertising segment warrants a digital ad multiple of 5x revenue. This mismatch creates systematic mispricing.

The methodology works best for:
- Conglomerates with diverse segments across multiple industries
- Holding companies with distinct subsidiaries
- Private equity portfolio companies requiring exit valuations
- Firms considering spin-offs
- Companies defending against hostile takeovers
- Biotech firms with multiple drug candidates at different clinical stages

## The SOTP Formula

Total Enterprise Value = Σ(Value of Individual Segments)
Equity Value = SOTP Enterprise Value - Net Debt - Non-Operating Liabilities + Non-Operating Assets
SOTP Value Per Share = Equity Value / Diluted Shares Outstanding

## Segment Identification through SEC Filings

US companies must disclose segment information under ASC 280, which requires public entities to report based on how the Chief Operating Decision Maker organises and evaluates the business internally.

Required disclosures include:
- External revenues for each reportable segment constituting more than 75% of consolidated revenue
- Segment profit or loss using the GAAP measure closest to management's internal reporting
- Total segment assets, segment depreciation and amortisation, segment capital expenditures

A segment becomes reportable if it meets any of three quantitative thresholds:
- Revenue must be 10% or more of combined revenue
- Profit or loss must be 10% or more of the greater of combined profits or combined losses
- Assets must be 10% or more of combined assets

## Individual Segment Valuation Methods

Three primary approaches:
1. Trading multiples relative to publicly traded peers
2. DCF analysis that discounts projected cash flows
3. Precedent transactions that apply multiples from recent M&A deals

Current EV/EBITDA multiples by sector (Damodaran data):
- Software: 27.98×
- Semiconductor: 34.48×
- Healthcare Products: 21.20×
- Aerospace & Defence: 17.24×
- Transportation/Railroad: 12.56×
- Retail General: 18.21×
- Oil & Gas Integrated: 6.70×
- Insurance P&C: 10.22× (but use P/B 0.8-1.8× instead)

For financial services: EV/EBITDA is inappropriate. Use Price-to-Book at 1.0-1.5× for banks and 1.0-2.0× for insurance, or Price-to-Earnings.

For high-growth technology not yet profitable: EV/Revenue is preferred.
- Software: 4× revenue
- Cloud: 8× revenue
- Semiconductors: 5× revenue

Identifying peer companies requires matching on:
- Industry or sub-sector
- Similar business model (B2B vs B2C)
- Comparable revenue growth profile within ±5%
- Similar margins within ±5% of EBITDA
- Comparable size within 0.5× revenue

## The Conglomerate Discount Phenomenon

The conglomerate discount represents the persistent gap between a diversified company's market capitalisation and its calculated SOTP value. Academic research dating back to Berger and Ofek's landmark 1995 study consistently documents an average discount of 13-15% in developed markets. This means conglomerates trade roughly $85 for every $100 of underlying segment value.

Formula: Discount = (SOTP Value - Market Cap) / SOTP Value × 100

Six primary factors drive this discount:
1. Inefficient capital allocation - strong segments cross-subsidising underperformers
2. Lack of management focus - executives cannot fully comprehend all aspects of diversified operations
3. Complexity and opacity - investors apply uncertainty discounts
4. Investor preference for pure-play exposure
5. Agency costs - management prioritises job security or empire-building over shareholder value
6. Internal subsidisation - resources diverted from high-return to low-return investments

The most important exception is Berkshire Hathaway, which trades at a persistent premium to SOTP at approximately 1.5× book value, demonstrating that exceptional management can overcome the conglomerate discount.

## Break-up Value Analysis and Famous Case Studies

### General Electric (2021-2024)
- November 2021: GE announced plans to form three public companies
- GE HealthCare spun off January 2023 (1 share for every 3 GE shares)
- GE Vernova spun off April 2024 (1 share for every 4 GE shares)
- Remaining entity: GE Aerospace
- Combined market value exceeds 4× the pre-break-up value
- GE Aerospace alone: $177.7B market cap, stock up 108% over 52 weeks

### Johnson & Johnson's Kenvue Spin-off (2023)
- May 2023 IPO at $22/share, valued at ~$41B
- Largest US IPO since Rivian
- J&J reduced outstanding shares by ~191M and generated $13.2B in cash

### Kellogg's Split (2023)
- October 2023: Split into Kellanova (global snacking, ~82%) and WK Kellogg (N.A. cereal, ~18%)
- Kellanova sold to Mars for ~$30B (August 2024)
- WK Kellogg acquired by Ferrero for $3.1B (January 2025)
- Combined acquisition value: $33.1B

## Holding Company Analysis and NAV Approach

Holding company analysis requires special considerations:
- Net Asset Value approach: value each underlying investment at market price for public holdings
- Use DCF and comparable analysis for private holdings
- Look-through valuation for operating subsidiaries separately from investment portfolio

## SOTP Limitations and Practical Considerations

SOTP overstates value when:
- Segments cannot actually operate independently
- Significant synergies would be lost in separation
- Transfer pricing artificially inflates segment profitability
- Separation costs and dis-synergies are ignored

Always apply a dis-synergy adjustment of 5-15% when modelling actual break-up scenarios.

Tax-free spin-off requirements under IRC Section 355:
- Distributing corp must own 80% of voting power and 80% of all other stock classes
- Transaction cannot be a device to distribute earnings as disguised dividends
- Both companies must be engaged in active business for at least 5 years prior
- Parent must distribute at least 80% of controlled corporation's stock

Conglomerate discount application:
- Larger discounts (15-30%): poor capital-allocation, limited transparency, value-destroying acquisitions
- Smaller discounts (5-10%): strong management, high transparency, demonstrated synergies
- No discount or premium: exceptional track records (Berkshire model)

Common mistakes:
- Failing to allocate corporate overhead (overstates value by 5-10%)
- Double-counting intercompany revenue
- Using inconsistent multiples across segments
- Ignoring minority interests
- Applying wrong peer groups
- Using stale comparable data
