## What is the Altman Z-Score?

The Altman Z-Score, designed by NYU Professor Edward Altman, is a model used to predict the near-term likelihood of companies falling into bankruptcy or insolvency.

## How to Calculate the Altman Z-Score (Step-by-Step)

Originally developed to evaluate the probability of bankruptcy within the manufacturing industry, the Altman z-score utilizes a weighting system of various financial ratios that each measure a specific financial attribute.

The purpose of the z-score model is to measure a company's financial health and quantify the probability of the company filing for bankruptcy or needing restructuring in the near future, i.e. within two years.

The five components:
- X1 = Working Capital ÷ Total Assets (short-term liquidity)
- X2 = Retained Earnings ÷ Total Assets (reliance on debt financing)
- X3 = EBIT ÷ Total Assets (operating profits from assets)
- X4 = Market Capitalization ÷ Total Liabilities (downside risk given insolvency risk)
- X5 = Sales ÷ Total Assets (sales generated compared to asset base)

Note: It is recommended for "Total Assets" to exclude any intangible assets.

## Altman Z-Score Formula

Altman Z-Score = (1.2 × X1) + (1.4 × X2) + (3.3 × X3) + (0.6 × X4) + (0.99 × X5)

Other model variations:
- Private Manufacturing Companies → Z-Score = 0.717 × X1 + 0.847 × X2 + 3.107 × X3 + 0.42 × X4 + 0.998 × X5
- Private General Non-Manufacturing Services Companies → Z-Score = 6.56 × X1 + 3.26 × X2 + 6.72 × X3 + 1.05 × X4
- Emerging Market Companies → Z-Score = 3.25 + 6.56 × X1 + 3.26 × X2 + 6.72 × X3 + 1.05 × X4

## How to Interpret Altman Z-Score

For public manufacturing companies:
- Z > 2.99: Safe Zone – Low Likelihood of Bankruptcy
- 1.81 to 2.99: Grey Zone – Moderate Risk of Bankruptcy
- Z < 1.81: Distress Zone – High Likelihood of Bankruptcy

For private non-manufacturing companies:
- Z > 2.60: Safe Zone – Low Likelihood of Bankruptcy
- 1.10 to 2.6: Grey Zone – Moderate Risk of Bankruptcy
- Z < 1.10: Distress Zone – High Likelihood of Bankruptcy

## Limitations

One of the main drawbacks is how abnormalities — not necessarily negative indicators — can result in a lower z-score.
- Restaurant industry: negative working capital cycle signifies strong cash flow management, not potential insolvency.
- Early-stage companies that are growing rapidly yet are unprofitable are not suited for the model.
- Must only be relied upon once deemed appropriate to the situation.

## Calculation Example

Suppose a public manufacturing company:
- Current Assets = $60M, Current Liabilities = $40M
- Fixed Assets = $100M, Net Income = $10M, Dividends = $2M
- Sales = $60M, COGS and SGA = $40M, P/E Multiple = 8.0x, Total Liabilities = $120M

Calculations:
- Working Capital = $60M - $40M = $20M
- Total Assets = $60M + $100M = $160M
- Retained Earnings = $10M - $2M = $8M
- EBIT = $60M - $40M = $20M
- Market Cap = 8.0x × $10M = $80M

X1 = 0.13, X2 = 0.05, X3 = 0.13, X4 = 0.67, X5 = 0.38

Z-Score = (1.20 × 0.13) + (1.40 × 0.05) + (3.30 × 0.13) + (0.60 × 0.67) + (0.99 × 0.38) = 1.40

Result: Distress Zone (Z < 1.81) — high risk of near-term insolvency.
