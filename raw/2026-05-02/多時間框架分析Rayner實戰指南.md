## What is Multi-Timeframe Analysis?

Looking at price movements across different timeframes to make smarter trading decisions. Instead of focusing on just one chart, you're zooming in and out, looking at both the big picture and the finer details.

It's like having both a telescope and microscope with you at the same time!

Using this technique, you get a much clearer idea of overall market trends, key support and resistance levels, and where the price might be headed.

## Why Use Multi-Timeframe Analysis

Example: You see a breakout on the 4-hour chart and think it's time to jump in...
But when you zoom out to the daily chart, the price is approaching a daily resistance level.
The price then rejects the daily resistance area and falls back.

MTA helps you avoid getting caught in a bad trade by syncing up with the overall trend and larger support and resistance zones.

## The Factor of 4 to 6 Rule

Take the lower timeframe you're trading on and multiply it by 4 to 6 to get your higher timeframe.

Examples:
- Trading on 1-hour chart → Use 4-hour chart for bigger picture
- Trading on 5-minute chart → Use 30-minute chart for bigger picture
- Trading on 15-minute chart → Use 1-hour chart for bigger picture

This approach helps you stay in sync with the overall trend while still getting the details you need for precise entries and exits.

However, traders working with lower timeframes may still find value in analyzing the daily and 4-hour charts for reference points.

## Higher Timeframe: What to Look For

The higher timeframe is like your map. It shows you key areas:
- Support and resistance levels
- Consolidation zones
- Previous highs and lows — places where price might react in the future

### Key uses of higher timeframe:
1. **Support/Resistance Zones**: Clearly defined zones acting as main magnets for price
2. **Trend Channels**: Price moving within channels shows where prices might continue heading
3. **Candlestick Formations**: Higher timeframe candlestick patterns hold more significance (e.g., inverted hammers on daily suggest significant rejection over several days)

## Lower Timeframe (Trading Timeframe): Aligning with Higher Timeframe

This is where you make your moves. But you've got to align your trades with what's happening on the higher timeframe.

Key strategy: Look for **stacked levels** — when support or resistance lines up on both your trading timeframe and the higher timeframe, that's a high-probability zone for the price to react.

### How price reacts at daily levels on lower timeframes:
When price returns to a magnet level on the daily chart, it often pauses and then continues in the direction of the trend. Strong bullish hammer candles at these levels provide great entry triggers.

## Break of Structure in the Direction of the Higher Timeframe

Always trade in the direction of the higher timeframe.

If the higher timeframe shows an uptrend, look for a break above a recent swing high on the lower timeframe to confirm that the trend is continuing.

For downtrend — wait for a break below a recent swing low.

This doesn't always present an immediate entry opportunity, but it provides a trading opportunity if the price makes a brief pullback.

## Price Patterns in Multi-Timeframe Analysis

Price patterns on higher timeframes (daily, weekly) carry more weight as they reflect broader market sentiment and involve a larger number of traders.

Classic patterns like head and shoulders, double tops, or triangles are more significant on higher timeframes.

If you notice a price pattern forming on a higher timeframe:
1. Don't enter immediately
2. Zoom in to a lower timeframe to fine-tune entries and exits
3. Wait for price to break a key level or confirm the reversal on the smaller timeframe

By aligning patterns across different timeframes, you increase your odds of success and avoid false signals from lower timeframes.

### Example: Head and Shoulders on 4-Hour Chart
- Identify the pattern on the 4-hour timeframe
- Jump to 1-hour chart to look for entry signals
- Price breaks the neckline on the 1-hour → entry opportunity
- Take profit: measured move of the neckline-to-head distance
- Stop loss: above the neckline where the trade becomes invalid

## Stacked Levels (Advanced Concept)

When major support and resistance zones line up across multiple timeframes — like an intersection where two roads meet, it's where price levels overlap.

If you see a resistance level forming on the 4-hour timeframe, but you're unsure if it will hold much weight, check the daily chart. If the daily also shows resistance at the same area, that's a stacked level — extremely high probability that price will react there.
