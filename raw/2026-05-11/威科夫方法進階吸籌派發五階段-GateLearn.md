## The Three Laws of the Wyckoff Method

### The Law of Supply and Demand

Wyckoff's law of supply and demand represents one of the most fundamental concepts in finance and economics. The law provides that the price action of an asset is dependent on changes in supply and demand:

- Demand > Supply: Increased price
- Demand < Supply: Decreased price
- Demand = Supply: Unchanged price

According to Wyckoff's principle, a bullish trend is created when more people buy than sell, thus hiking the market demand over supply. Conversely, where people are selling more than buying, demand falls, and supply rises, causing a price drop.

This principle is usually applied by considering volume data and comparing it to price action. By understanding the relationship between supply and demand for the current market, traders can come up with insight into the upcoming market directions.

### The Law of Cause and Effect

Wyckoff's second law pertains to the differences in supply and demand. The law of Cause and Effect argues that there is an underlying logic to the differences in supply and demand.

The principle provides that mass trading action results in disparities in supply and demand. A market period dominated by buying (accumulation) will result in a rise in demand, raised prices, and ultimately bring about an uptrend. Conversely, where the market is collectively selling (distribution), there will be an increase in supply, thereby causing the reduced prices that are apparent in a downtrend.

Wyckoff applied this principle in predicting how long a market trend would last after a period of consolidation.

### The Law of Effort vs. Result

Wyckoff's third law proposes that the trading volume should represent changes in the price of an asset. The law of effort vs. result posits that where the trading volume does not reflect the price action of an asset, there exists a variation in the market trend. This means that when an asset's trading volume and price action are in harmony, the market trend is a dominant one and is likely to continue. Conversely, if there are differences between the volume and price action, the trend will likely end soon or reverse.

## Wyckoff's Theory on the Market Cycle

According to Wyckoff, the four phases of a market cycle are Accumulation, Markup, Distribution, and Markdown.

### Accumulation

The first stage is characterized by sideways price movement. Big players buy in carefully and skillfully at a rate small enough to avoid attention. As more traders develop interest, the trading range features higher lows, with prices preparing to push higher. As the bulls assume power, prices break through the upper level of the trading range, establishing a consistent uptrend.

### Markup

The consistent uptrend established at the end of the accumulation phase persists. Less experienced traders start buying in, driving demand up. Some experienced traders might exit positions. During Markup, there may be multiple shorter phases of accumulation (re-accumulation), where the larger uptrend somewhat pauses before continuing.

### Distribution

Once the price action fails to set new highs, distribution begins. The former uptrend loses momentum and the market moves sideways. Traders and investors start selling off assets. Less experienced investors continue acquiring the asset in hopes of a new uptrend. Price action marks lower tops and higher lows, signaling a trend reversal.

### Markdown

The last stage kicks off with a downtrend. Traders and investors rush to close positions. The bearish phase is established when prices fall past the lows of the distribution phase. Markdown ends when the base signals the start of a new accumulation phase.

## Wyckoff Accumulation Schematics: Events

### Preliminary Support (PS)
First event of accumulation phase. Occurs after a long bearish trend. Volume increases and price spread widens, pointing to the bear trend approaching a halt.

### Selling Climax (SC)
Comes after PS, with significant price drop. Features heavy or panicked selling, and the price closes off very low in a long candlestick.

### Automatic Rally (AR)
Selling pressure from SC is close to non-existent, and a small wave of buying eases the price upwards. Short sellers often dominate this event, covering their positions. The high of AR determines the high of the accumulation trading range.

### Secondary Test (ST)
Price revisits the low set by SC at a controlled stage. Characterized by higher volumes and price spread.

### Spring
Features a hard test of concurrent lows that often mislead traders. Some traders will judge the spring as a bearish trend and launch into selling. The spring ends with a shakeout.

### Sign of Strength (SOS)
Price advances at increasing speed, supported by relatively higher volume. Usually occurs after spring to confirm interpretation of prior market action.

### Last Point of Support (LPS)
Final pullback in the accumulation phase before buildup for a bullish trend. May occur more than once despite the finality of the name.

## Wyckoff Accumulation Schematics: Phases

### Phase A
First phase kickoffs just as prior downtrend ends. Before this phase, supply dominated. PS and SC occur, signaling beginning of accumulation. AR and ST close off the phase.

### Phase B
According to law of cause and effect, phase B is the cause leading to the upcoming uptrend. Large players build interest. Consolidation phase featuring varying levels of resistance and support. Multiple STs, wide price swings, high volume. Eventually supply balances with demand, volume reduces, signaling onset of phase C.

### Phase C
Spring occurs, putting asset through decisive test of remaining supply. Price moves below support level and back into TR. Spring is last bear trap before price starts climbing. If successful, represents high trading opportunity, validated by SOS.

### Phase D
Market transitions from Cause to Effect. Features increased volume and volatility. Price moves to top of TR. LPS occurs at this stage — good points to enter long position.

### Phase E
Stock leaves TR and begins ascent. Demand dominates with short-lived setbacks and shakeouts.
