# McGinley Dynamic Indicator: What It Is and How to Use It

The McGinley Dynamic Indicator is an advanced type of moving average that adjusts for shifts in market speed. Think of it as an MA with a filter, smoothing price data to avoid whipsaws.

This technical analysis tool was designed to solve the main issue with moving averages — their reliance on fixed time periods. It helps solve the issue of varying market speeds and provides a more accurate reflection of price action.

The McGinley Dynamic indicator was invented by John R. McGinley, a former editor of the Market Technicians Association's Journal of Technical Analysis and a Certified Market Technician. It was first published in the Journal of Technical Analysis in 1997.

Many traders, analysts, and market experts refer to the McGinley Dynamic Indicator as "the most reliable indicator you have never heard of."

## The Theory Behind the McGinley Dynamic

There are two main types of moving averages: SMA and EMA. The SMA takes past closing prices and divides them by the number of periods to smooth out the price action. The EMA puts more weight on the latest periods.

What both indicators fail to do is to adjust for the speed of the market. This leaves traders wondering whether to use a 10-day, 20-day, or 50-day moving average in both fast and slow markets.

McGinley wanted to change that by designing an indicator that adjusts to the current market speed. His end goal was to ensure that the indicator's line "hugs" the price line. Doing so would reduce price separation and whipsaws in both fast and slow markets.

Another important thing is that the McGinley Dynamic indicator overcomes the lag from the fixed-period time model that standard moving averages employ.

## McGinley Dynamic Formula and Calculation

MDi = MDi-1 + [Close – MDi-1 / N × (Close / MDi-1)⁴]

Where:
- MDi – Current McGinley Dynamic
- MDi-1 – Previous McGinley Dynamic
- Close – Closing price
- N – the MA period

Alternative version with "k" parameter:
MDi = MDi-1 + [Close – MDi-1 / k × N × (Close / MDi-1)⁴]
Where k = 60% of the selected period N.

The way the indicator is calculated defines one of its notable characteristics: it speeds up in bear markets and slows down in bull markets.

## How to Use

### Spotting an uptrend
Price crosses the indicator line from below and remains above it. The indicator line should continue its steady upward movement.

### Spotting a downtrend
Price crosses the McGinley Dynamic line from above and remains below it while the indicator proceeds its downward movement.

### Spotting a trend reversal
An excessive deviation between the price bars and the indicator's line signals a potential trend reversal.

## McGinley Dynamic vs. Other Indicators

### vs. Moving Averages (SMA, EMA, WMA)
SMA, EMA, and WMA fail to adjust for market speed. They provide isolated price information without market dynamics. The McGinley Dynamic adjusts to market speed shifts and acknowledges market acceleration and deceleration.

### McGinley Dynamic + MACD
When applied on a chart, the McGinley Dynamic smooths the line that traditional EMAs usually generate. Meanwhile, the MACD helps confirm a long-term trend. Traders wait until the MACD crosses the signal line, and the candle body closes on the same end of the McGinley Dynamic for at least two consecutive trading periods.

### McGinley Dynamic + RSI
Instead of looking for RSI breaking 30 and 70 marks, focus on RSI movement around the 50-line. If price closes above McGinley Dynamic and RSI is above 50, it's a buy signal. If price closes below McGinley Dynamic and RSI is below 50, it's a sell signal.

## Key Takeaways
- McGinley Dynamic is superior to most comparable technical trading tools in reducing whipsaws
- It speeds up in bear markets and slows down in bull markets
- Think of it as a car with automatic transmission — adjusts gears based on speed
- McGinley himself advised using it as a smoothing mechanism, not a signal generator
