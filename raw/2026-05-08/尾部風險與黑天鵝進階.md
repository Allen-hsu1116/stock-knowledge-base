## Tail Risk (Wikipedia)

Tail risk, sometimes called "fat tail risk", is the financial risk of an asset or portfolio of assets moving more than three standard deviations from its current price, above the risk of a normal distribution. Tail risks include low-probability events arising at both ends of a normal distribution curve, also known as tail events. However, as investors are generally more concerned with unexpected losses rather than gains, the debate about tail risk is focused on the left tail.

The common technique of theorizing a normal distribution of price changes underestimates tail risk when market data exhibit fat tails, thus understating asset prices, stock returns and subsequent risk management strategies.

Tail risk is sometimes defined less strictly: as merely the risk (or probability) of rare events.

Although tail risk cannot be eliminated, its impact can be somewhat mitigated by a robust diversification across assets, strategies, and the use of an asymmetric hedge.

### Characteristics of tail risk

Traditional portfolio strategies rely heavily upon the assumption that market returns follow a normal distribution. The empirical rule then states that about 99.7% of all variations following a normal distribution lies within three standard deviations of the mean. Therefore, there is only a 0.3% chance of an extreme event occurring.

However, financial markets are not perfect as they are largely shaped by unpredictable human behavior and an abundance of evidence suggests that the distribution of returns is in fact not normal, but skewed. Observed tails are fatter than traditionally predicted, indicating a significantly higher probability that an investment will move beyond three standard deviations.

These tail events are often referred to as black swan events and they can produce disastrous effects on the returns of the portfolio in a very short span of time.

McRandal and Rozanov (2012) observe that from the late 1980s to the early 2010s, there were at least seven episodes that can be viewed as tail events: equity market crash of 1987, 1994 bond market crisis, 1997 Asian financial crisis, 1998 Russian financial crisis and the LTCM blow-up, dot-com bubble collapse, subprime mortgage crisis, and Lehman Brothers bankruptcy.

### Role of the 2008 financial crisis

The 2008 financial crisis and the Great Recession led to a significant increase in awareness of tail risks. Losses of many broadly diversified, multi-asset class portfolios ranged anywhere from 20% to 30% in the course of just a few months.

A true tail event should exhibit the following three properties simultaneously with significant magnitude and speed: falling asset prices, increasing risk premia, and increasing correlations between asset classes.

### Actively managed tail hedge strategies

Active tail risk managers identify possible epicenters of tail events, construct the hedge, and guarantee constant effectiveness of optimal protection by active trading of positions. When all steps are combined, alpha can be generated. Active management minimizes 'negative carry' and provides sufficient ongoing security and a truly convex payoff delivered in tail events. Furthermore, it manages to mitigate counterparty risk.

## Black Swan Theory (Wikipedia)

The black swan theory describes an event that comes as a surprise, has a major effect, and is often inappropriately rationalized after the fact with the benefit of hindsight.

Taleb defines the term as an event with two characteristics:
1. It is so rare and outside the realm of expectations that it is unpredictable
2. Its consequences are extreme—either beneficial or catastrophic

Taleb provides the example of the 9/11 attacks, which were a black swan for many, but not for its planners and perpetrators.

Taleb's black swan theory refers only to statistically unexpected events of large magnitude and consequence and their dominant role in history.

### Coping with black swans

The practical aim is not to attempt to predict events which are unpredictable, but to build robustness against negative events while still exploiting positive events. Taleb contends that banks and trading firms are very vulnerable to hazardous black swan events.

Taleb elaborates the robustness concept as a central topic of his later book, Antifragile: Things That Gain From Disorder.

Taleb states that a black swan event depends on the observer. What may be a Black Swan surprise for a turkey is not a Black Swan surprise to its butcher; hence the objective should be to "avoid being the turkey" by identifying areas of vulnerability to "turn the Black Swans white".

### Categories of events (Barth & Schreft 2025)

- **Black Swan**: Near 24/7 pervasive media coverage; major new legislation; reorganisation of industries; birth of new subindustries
- **Grey Swan**: Extensive coverage, "big event reminiscent of the last time"; targeted interventions; new products/services to hedge
- **White Swan**: Brief coverage, most significant of the month; limited adjustment of existing agreements; updating practices
- **Duck**: Lead segment for a week; business as usual; little to no interest

COVID-19 pandemic: Ahmad, Kutan, and Gupta consider it a black swan. Ghosh and van den Heiligenberg label it a "grey swan." Taleb calls it a "white swan"—something with great impact that will occur with great certainty.

## Antifragile (Taleb)

Antifragility is defined as a convex response to a stressor or source of harm, leading to a positive sensitivity to increase in volatility. Likewise, fragility is defined as a concave sensitivity to stressors, leading to a negative sensitivity to an increase in volatility.

Taleb's thesis: in a volatile world with destructive uncertainty, the wise economic strategy is to be antifragile: protect the downside but prepare to benefit disproportionately from potential external negative events and in all cases avoid fragility.

### Barbell Strategy

A dual strategy, a combination of two extremes, one safe and one speculative, deemed more robust than a "monomodal" strategy; often a necessary condition for antifragility.

Example: marrying an accountant (stable life) and having an occasional fling with a rock star (good fun). For a writer, getting a stable sinecure and writing without the pressures of the market during spare time.

Even trial and error are a form of barbell.

### Via Negativa

Focus on what to avoid or what not to do, rather than what to do. Removing offensive irritants rather than adding remedies.

### Green Lumber Fallacy

Mistaking one important kind of knowledge for another—focusing on theoretical understanding rather than practical, domain-specific knowledge that actually drives outcomes. A successful green lumber trader thought "green lumber" was painted green rather than freshly cut, yet still made a fortune trading it. Meanwhile, the intellectually sophisticated narrator went bust.

### Skin in the Game

Every opinion maker needs to have "skin in the game" in the event of harm caused by reliance on his information or opinion. Anyone producing a forecast or making an economic analysis needs to have something to lose from it.
